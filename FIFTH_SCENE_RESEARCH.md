# Fifth Scene Research Draft

## Working Title

`智慧農場全球任務站`

This is a research-first draft for the fifth scene. It is not yet the final
scene script. The purpose of this file is to organize the subject matter that
should shape the next playable scenario.

## Why This Scene Matters

Compared with the earlier scenes, smart agriculture should not be framed only
as "technology applied to farming." A stronger framing is:

- food systems under climate pressure
- water scarcity and irrigation efficiency
- electricity and energy trade-offs
- farm management and return on investment
- smallholder adoption barriers
- data governance and market fairness
- future resilience of global agrifood systems

This makes the scene both locally relevant and globally meaningful.

## Recommended Scene Positioning

Player role:

- smart farm mission planner
- or smart agriculture operations assistant

Core message:

- smart farming is not just about installing sensors
- it is about making agriculture more efficient, resilient, equitable, and
  sustainable under real-world pressures

Narrative tone:

- more strategic than the previous scenes
- more global than the previous scenes
- still playable for students, but with stronger system-thinking

## Research Modules

### 1. Core Technology Module

This module should provide the technical foundation of the scene.

Suggested topics:

- soil moisture sensing
- temperature and humidity monitoring
- weather stations
- smart irrigation control
- remote sensing and satellite data
- drone inspection
- crop image recognition
- disease and pest monitoring
- AI-based prediction and advisory systems
- farm dashboards and alerts
- traceability and digital records

Design use:

- image-choice questions
- matching questions
- scenario-based single-choice questions

### 2. Business and Commercial Module

This module explains why smart agriculture is not only a technical story.

Suggested topics:

- labour shortages and aging agricultural workforce
- quality consistency and yield stability
- premium crops versus bulk crops
- return on investment and payback period
- postharvest handling and food safety
- market access and price information
- traceability as a commercial advantage

Official background signals:

- FAO notes that digital agriculture can improve efficiency and productivity,
  and address bottlenecks in food safety, postharvest handling, market access,
  finance, and supply chain management.
- World Bank notes that digital agriculture can improve yields, reduce food
  loss and waste, and help farmers receive fair pay by reducing information
  asymmetries and transaction costs.

Design use:

- branching questions where the player must decide how a farm should invest
- business background questions instead of pure device identification

### 3. Adoption Barriers and Promotion Challenges Module

This module is essential if the scene is meant to reflect reality.

Suggested topics:

- high upfront equipment cost
- uncertain business returns
- smallholder affordability
- digital divide
- connectivity and platform access
- maintenance and after-sales barriers
- fragmented crop / soil / water research ecosystems
- lack of local training and extension support
- weak data governance and data-rights protection
- reluctance to share data

Official background signals:

- FAO highlights the need to bridge the digital divide, which still excludes
  many small farmers, and to make the business environment more conducive for
  providers.
- FAO climate-smart agriculture guidance notes that adoption depends on the
  broader economic, social, and environmental context, and that integrated
  investigation is needed to understand the constraints farmers face.
- World Bank highlights obstacles such as unequal technological
  infrastructure, incompatibility between data sources, privacy concerns, and
  proprietary systems.
- World Bank also notes that farmers may be reluctant to share data because of
  privacy, security, ownership, and legal uncertainty.

Design use:

- branching questions
- timed decision questions
- "why is推广 hard?" style questions

### 4. Water and Irrigation Module

This should be one of the signature pillars of the fifth scene.

Official background signals:

- FAO states that agriculture withdraws roughly 70 percent of the world’s
  freshwater.
- FAO also states that more than 60 percent of global irrigated cropland is
  under high water stress.
- FAO emphasizes that irrigation improvements must be paired with better water
  governance, water rights, and better water data.
- FAO also notes that irrigation can support adaptation, save labour and
  energy, and allow the production of higher-value crops.

Suggested topics:

- soil moisture monitoring
- precision irrigation
- variable-rate irrigation
- water stress and drought response
- water accounting
- water rights and equitable access
- satellite-supported water management
- "more crop per drop" style efficiency logic

Design use:

- ordering questions about irrigation decision flow
- matching questions between devices and water-management outcomes
- scenario questions on water scarcity

### 5. Electricity and Energy Module

This is another key pillar, especially because smart farming often adds
automation, sensing, pumping, cooling, communication, and analytics loads.

Useful direction from official material:

- FAO explicitly links improved irrigation with labour and energy savings.
- This supports a scene design where smart agriculture is evaluated not only
  by yield or convenience, but also by resource efficiency.

Suggested topics:

- energy costs of pumping and irrigation
- greenhouse and environment-control power demand
- cold-chain and storage electricity use
- energy-efficient scheduling
- solar plus smart farm equipment
- balancing automation benefits with energy needs

Design use:

- trade-off questions
- "save water but use more power?" decision questions
- future-development questions on renewable integration

### 6. Global Outlook and Future Development Module

This module is what makes the scene global rather than only local.

Official background signals:

- FAO states that agrifood systems are under pressure from climate shocks,
  conflict, and economic volatility while the world must feed a projected 9.7
  billion people by 2050.
- FAO highlights the triple divide: digital, rural, and gender.
- World Bank frames digital agriculture as part of a broader transformation
  toward a more efficient, equitable, and environmentally sustainable food
  system.

Suggested topics:

- food security under climate change
- resilience and climate-smart agriculture
- smallholder inclusion
- digital data and AI governance
- platform interoperability
- equitable access to agritech
- future farm talent and cross-domain skills

Design use:

- late-stage scenario questions
- final "why this matters globally" questions
- role-card outcomes that include both technology and stewardship

## Recommended Knowledge Structure For Questions

I recommend a 10-question structure instead of a short 6- or 7-question scene.

Suggested sequence:

1. smart agriculture core concept
2. farm technology identification
3. water-management decision
4. irrigation or drought-response quick decision
5. energy-use trade-off question
6. business / ROI background question
7. smallholder adoption barrier question
8. data / platform / governance question
9. future development and global food-system question
10. final synthesis question

This will make the scene clearly different from the earlier ones and better
reflect the topic depth the user requested.

## Recommended Result Card Directions

I recommend four result roles for this scene:

- `智慧灌溉調度員`
- `農場數據觀察員`
- `農業系統整合師`
- `永續農業策劃者`

These roles map well onto:

- water
- data
- systems
- long-term strategy

## Candidate Question-Type Mix

Recommended mix:

- `single-choice`
- `timed-choice`
- `image-choice`
- `matching`
- `ordering`
- `branching`

Suggested use of branching:

- choose between farm-investment priorities
- respond to drought or water shortage
- decide whether to prioritize yield, water saving, energy use, or cost

This will make the player feel like they are making farm management decisions,
not just answering technology trivia.

## Research Notes By Theme

### Technology

- Focus on technologies students can visualize:
  sensors, irrigation controllers, drones, dashboards, cameras, weather data,
  and remote sensing.

### Business

- Do not frame smart agriculture as "install devices and everything is solved."
- Include questions about why farms adopt technology, what problems they are
  trying to solve, and which investments are easier or harder to justify.

### Promotion Challenges

- Keep a strong smallholder perspective.
- Make sure the scene acknowledges affordability, training, and trust.

### Water

- Make water a headline issue, not a side note.
- This is one of the best ways to connect local farming concerns with global
  sustainability concerns.

### Energy

- Avoid treating electricity as invisible background.
- Use it as a real decision dimension in the scene.

### Global Future

- Make at least one late-stage question connect local farm decisions with
  climate resilience and global food security.
